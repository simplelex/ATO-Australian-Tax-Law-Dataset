SimpleLex Point-in-Time Explorer

ATO Tax Law, as it stood.

Read any ATO-administered Act as it was in force on a chosen date, and compare it against the current law — provision by provision.

How this explorer works

A plain-language window into how Australian tax law has changed over time — letting you read any ATO-administered Act exactly as it stood on a date you choose, and see what has changed since.

What this is for

When you need to apply the law as it was at a particular moment — a transaction, an income year, a dispute — the current Act is not enough. This tool rebuilds each Act’s history so you can pick a date, read the provision as it then stood, and compare it side-by-side with today’s wording, with the amendment that caused each change attached.

Where the data comes from

Everything is built from the Federal Register of Legislation (legislation.gov.au) — the official, authoritative Commonwealth source for Acts and their amendment history. We cover the set of Acts administered by the ATO.

The text and amendment facts are Crown copyright, reused under CC BY 4.0. The history notes here are our own plain wording reconstructed from the Register’s official endnotes.

How the point-in-time text is built

Each time an Act is amended, the Register publishes a new compilation — a fresh consolidated version of the whole Act — stamped with the date it came into force. We:

  1. list every compilation of the Act and the date range each one was in force;
  2. download the full text of each compilation and split it into individual provisions (sections, subsections, and so on);
  3. store each provision’s text against the dates that version applied.

To show the law as at your chosen date, the tool finds the compilation that was in force on that date and displays that provision’s text. Compare to current then diffs it against the latest compilation.

How the history notes are built

Every compilation carries official endnotes listing what changed. We:

  1. read the legislation-history endnote (which amending Act made changes, and when each commenced);
  2. read the amendment-history endnote (which provision was added, amended, repealed or substituted);
  3. rebuild, in plain wording, a dated note for each change and attach it to the provision it affected — matched to the version it produced by its commencement date.

Open any provision and choose Show full history to see every version in order, the dates it applied, a visual diff against the version before it, and the amendment note(s) behind each change. The strip above the provision list does the same at the whole-Act level.

Pinpointing the amendment — the Schedule item

An amending Act makes its changes through a numbered list of Schedule items — item 1 changes this, item 2 inserts that. Where we can, each history note names the exact item responsible — for example “Amended by No 101 of 2008, Sch 2 item 1” — by opening the amending Act and finding the item that names the provision being changed. That gives you a precise paper trail from the change back into the Act that made it.

Some notes carry no item, and that is faithful to the source. A newly inserted section is identified in the amending Act only by where it goes (“after section 110‑55”), never by its own future number; and some changes are made indirectly — editing a table or a whole Subdivision — without printing the section number. In those cases the note still records what changed and which Act did it, and leaves the item out.

When text can’t be shown — and why

A small number of versions show “No consolidated text published for this version” instead of text. This is a limit of the official record, not a gap in the law — and in every case the change itself is still described by the attached note. The reasons:

  • Timeline-only changes (the vast majority). An amending Act commenced on that date, but the Federal Register never registered a separate consolidated compilation for it — there is no document of any kind (no web, Word or PDF) to display. The in-force text shown is the previous compilation’s, and the attached note describes exactly what changed.
  • Registered in Word/PDF only. A couple of versions were registered as Word/PDF without a structured web/epub version we can read automatically. For these the “View the registered Word/PDF on the Federal Register” link takes you straight to the official document.
  • Very old layouts. A tiny fraction of the oldest digitised compilations use a pre-2006 layout that doesn’t separate cleanly, so a few provisions there may show blank text; the “Source: Federal Register” link on each provision opens the original.

Wherever a document does exist on the Federal Register, every provision and every version carries a “Source: Federal Register” link to the official record, so you can always verify the text against the authorised source.

About the formatting

The source stores each provision as one continuous block of text. To make it readable, the tool rebuilds the legislative structure and indents it:

  • subsections (1), paragraphs (a), subparagraphs (i) and sub-subparagraphs (A) are nested by level;
  • method-statement Steps and Note/Example lines are set out on their own;
  • cross-references inside the text (e.g. “paragraph 5(2)(b)”) are left untouched — only genuine structure is indented.

In a comparison, inserted wording is highlighted and removed wording is struck through, matched up provision-item by provision-item so you see exactly what moved.
